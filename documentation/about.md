Not all demos run with a MP3 soundtrack. The term SoundTracker itself, as well as the music format, was initially designed by Karsten Obarski in 1987. This format can be schematically described a combination of musical sequence data and a set of instruments stored as audio samples. In tracker files produced with SoundTracker-derived software, whether on Amiga, Atari ST or PC platforms, exists an internal signature space, showing, in the most widespread MOD variants, a list of 31 samples slots allowed by the format. This internal textual space, embedded in the file itself, was originally meant for musicians to name their sampled instruments (violin, bass, kick, snare). In his works, Takashi Kawano explains how this space, both technical and textual, was repurposed in various ways to sign, credit, comment or contextualize the musical composition, allowing greetings, jokes and personal messages to appear within the file. Without explicitly treating them as discussions in the strict sense, these traces of social dynamics found in these musical artefacts can complement both the phenomenological and discursive study of the audio side of the demoscene. We therefore propose to question the possible contribution of artificial intelligence to the annotation of these meta-discursives traces, embedded within the sound layer of pre-2000 demoscene.
